EU H2020

ID: SC1-BHC-34-2020 - Specific Challenge:

  • Antimicrobial resistance represents a serious threat to public health in Europe and beyond.

  • Within the last decades resistance has increased considerably in many clinically important pathogenic bacteria.

  • Data collected by the European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control (ECDC)[1] shows that nowadays in several European countries prevalence levels of infections that can no longer be treated with last-line classes of antibiotics have reached levels where isolation measures may no longer be feasible.

EU H2020

ID: SC1-BHC-34-2020 - Specific Challenge:

  • In addition to this, prevalence levels of resistant infections are likely to increase in countries where currently such levels are relatively low. This may lead to an increasing number of outbreaks of resistant infections in these countries.

  • The challenge is to address this threat via a multi-disciplinary approach by developing suitable clinical management and infection prevention plans detailing how to deal with resistant bacterial infections in high prevalence settings.

EU H2020

ID: SC1-BHC-34-2020 - Specific Challenge:

  • The spread of AMR across borders has been recognised globally and improving knowledge on clinical management and infection prevention in high prevalence settings might also benefit other countries around the globe, including low and middle income countries and thereby diminish the spread of resistant bacteria.

  • This topic will contribute to the implementation of the EU One Health Action Plan against Antimicrobial Resistance[2].

Multiresistance

  • Different from simple resistance. >= 3 atb families

  • Many mechanisms

    • Enzymatic: encoded in plasmids. Transmisible
    • Chromosomal: non transmisible
    • …

  • Plasmids: DNA strands with encoded resistance

    • Persistence. Resistant to degradation
    • Spread to the environment
    • Water, soil
    • Hosts: gut bacteria

  • Prion-like behaviour.

Plasmids

Useful concepts:

  • DNA strands. Can be left outside bacteria and survive in the environment.

  • Defensive. Not offensive. No pathogenic.

  • Bacteria get “infected” by them.

    • This Enhances their defensive phenotype.

    • But at a Cost: energy spent building, maintaining defensive proteins, pumping antibiotics…

    • Often this diminishes their offensive capabilities.

Plasmids

  • Produced and successfully transmitted under antibiotic pressure.

    • Farms: mammal, avian, fish.
    • Agriculture.
    • Increased growth, able to increase survival at higher animal concentrations.
    • AMR are naturalised in animal guts.

  • Released into environment, food.

  • Picked up by human gut bacteria. Naturalised. Eventual infections (gut, urinary) will be by AMR.

    • Inadequate empirical treatment with disease progression to sepsis.
    • Death by uncontrolled bacterial spread or bacterial toxemia (pre-antibiotic era).

Plasmid mapping

  • Gut tract swabs, cultured.

  • Inexpensive at scale. Non-invasive for humans and animals.

  • Routine in selected human groups to monitor colonisation.

  • People starting to do it in animals.

Plasmid prediction

  • If we found a way to predict plasmids:

    • Restrict sampling areas in future studies.
    • Establish permanent monitoring in risk areas. (Like tuberculin). Animals, and humans.
    • Design, evaluate interventions to curb plasmid production/spreading.
    • Change european policies regarding ATBs in farms.

Tasks: Human, hospital

  • Routine anal swabs (RAS) in patients who have multiresistance risk criteria. Standard procedure involving a nurse and the Microbiology Department.

  • Data collection from cultured swabs from colonised/uncolonised individuals. Involvement of people/partner for data collection, curation and custody.

    • Bacteria type.
    • Multiresistance / lack thereof.
    • Pacient address ZIP code.
    • Type of address (home, nursing-home).
    • Recent hospital admission.
    • Antibiotic pressure: antibiotic usage history in last year.
    • Demographic data.
    • Mortality.
    • Hospital length of stay.

Tasks: Human, community

  • Random RAS sampling in outpatients attending to any health facility. Sample is self-collected by the patient. Needs training by a nurse.

    • Requires nurse for kit delivery, patient training and sample collection.
    • Person for the recovery and transport of samples to the Microbiology Department in tertiary hospital.
    • Antibiotic pressure: antibiotic usage history in last year (needs database).

  • Random RAS sampling in healthy population.

  • Number of kits needed: [unknown].

  • Areas to sample: [unknown].

Example: the problem of colistin resistance:

  • Currently, there’s chromosomic resistance to colistin, which is not transmissible.
  • This is why the intense colistin usage in chickens does not seem to impact (yet) colistin resistance in human populations.
  • In Asia, MCR-1 plasmidic colistin resistance (transmissible!) has emerged (pig farms) but not reached Europe yet [@carroll_identification_2019].

Objectives

  • Prevalence and spatial distribution of antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) in [two/three] European areas.

  • Determine the importance (global prevalence and impact in human antibiotic resistance) of the different AMR mechanisms.

  • Quantify the transmission of the most important AMR mechanisms between animal and human populations via:

    • Spatial proximity and levels of exposure. Vertical spread.
    • Different epidemiological vectors: food, soil, water.
    • Usage in horticulture: impact on soil.
    • Unclear how could we monitor food.

Objectives

  • Predict the probability of a given AMR at a given location.

    • Possibility of considering a time-series of AMR appearance at a given location?.
    • AMR: actually a binary variable.
    • The environmental pressure for a bacteria to adopt a AMR plaasmid may be more important than the density of such plasmid in the environment (to a point).

  • Design a series of (small scale) interventions to reduce important AMRs.

  • Informed assessment of the impact of different interventions to reduce probability of AMR in animal farms and human populations.

  • Identification of a link between AMR in livestock and human populations, and relationship with respective antibiotic histories and current usage practices
